## The opportunity
27,100/month at KD 38 on the main term, with an entire cluster of related searches totaling 50k+/month. The SERP is bifurcated: Harvard / Healthline on one side (seed oils are fine, keep your Mediterranean diet), Paul Saladino / trad-food Substacks on the other (seed oils are poison). **Nobody** in the top 10 does the tripartite framework (linoleic acid ratio vs high-heat oxidation vs inflammation-marker data) that would actually resolve the debate. This is a high-prestige post — done right, it gets cited by both sides.

## Search intent
- Primary intent: informational, but the reader is often trying to resolve conflicting content they've already encountered
- Dominant SERP format: long explainer with section headings for each sub-argument
- Average word count of top 5: 2,800
- People Also Ask:
  - What are the worst seed oils?
  - Is canola oil really bad?
  - Why do restaurants use seed oils?
  - Is olive oil heated bad?
  - What oil should I cook with?
- Featured snippet target: 80-word summary of the tripartite framework

## Required structure
- H1 with primary keyword (rephrased as the question)
- 80-word direct answer: three separate claims, which ones are supported, which aren't, our headline position
- H2: The three claims most people conflate
  - H3: Layer 1 — Linoleic acid ratio changes (historical intake rise, Blasbalg 2011 data)
  - H3: Layer 2 — Oxidation from repeated high-heat frying (real concern, the Vladykovskaya data)
  - H3: Layer 3 — "Inflammation" from linoleic acid (weak human evidence, Johnson & Fritsche 2012 meta-analysis, Hooper 2020 Cochrane review)
- H2: The cardiovascular literature (Mozaffarian 2010 meta-analysis, the PREDIMED followups, the current AHA position)
- H2: Butter and saturated fat — what the current evidence shows (brief, because this isn't a "saturated fat" post)
- H2: Olive oil — the polyphenol + oxidation story
- H2: The actual cooking decision matrix (smoke points, oxidation stability, cost per serving)
- H2: Our position, stated plainly
- H2: What's in your kitchen this week — a practical swap list
- FAQ section

## Required citations
- Blasbalg et al. 2011 (*Am J Clin Nutr*) — linoleic acid intake trend
- Vladykovskaya et al. 2012 (*J Biol Chem*) — oxidation products from heated oils
- Johnson & Fritsche 2012 (*Nutr Rev*) — meta-analysis on linoleic acid and inflammation
- Hooper et al. 2020 Cochrane review on dietary fats
- Mozaffarian et al. 2010 (*PLoS Med*) — polyunsaturated fat CVD meta-analysis
- Ramsden et al. 2013 (*BMJ*) — the Sydney Diet Heart Study reanalysis
- Saladino / Teicholz position pieces (cited as "dissent," not as authorities)

## Pinterest pin angles
1. "Seed oils: the 3 claims people conflate (and which ones the data supports)"
2. "Your cooking oil decision matrix, ranked"
3. "Restaurant frying vs your kitchen salad — the oxidation story"
4. "Save: the seed oil argument, resolved"

## Editor notes
- **This post has to be fair to both sides.** If we read as industry-captured, the seed oil audience dismisses us. If we read as crunchy-panic, the mainstream audience dismisses us. Neither can happen.
- State our position explicitly once we've walked through the evidence. Don't "both sides" it to the point of uselessness.
- The cooking oil decision matrix is the post's reusable artifact — screenshot-ready table. Columns: oil, smoke point, oxidation stability, polyphenol content, $/serving, recommended use cases.
- Paul Saladino, Chris Masterjohn, Max Lugavere — cite them, but frame as "popular positions" not "authorities." Similarly, Harvard's Nutrition Source gets cited as "institutional position."
- Do NOT use the phrase "industrial seed oils." Use "refined seed oils" when precision matters. "Industrial" is the trad-food coded word and it signals team alignment.
- The ending recommendation should be boring and correct: extra-virgin olive oil for most cooking, butter/ghee for high-heat sautéing if you like the flavor, avoid deep-frying oils, stop worrying about the salad dressing at a restaurant.
