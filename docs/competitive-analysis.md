# Larderlab Competitive Analysis

Positioning thesis: **Examine.com meets Marie Kondo for food.** Systems, not recipes. Frameworks, not feelings. Lab-coded voice for the male-skew optimizer 28-45 who listens to Attia, logs into Cronometer at 9pm, and wants the *decision algorithm*, not a "5 Easy Weeknight Dinners" listicle.

This document audits the live Google US SERP for six Wave 1 keywords, profiles the ten most relevant competitors in the evidence-led food space, and lists ten positioning moves larderlab can ship that nobody in the top 10 is doing.

Analyst: Claude. Date: 2026-04-20. Method: live Google SERP via WebSearch + competitor audits.

## Section 1: Live SERP audits (Wave 1 keywords)

### 1.1 "how much protein per day" / "protein intake calculator"

**Live top 10 (observed):**
1. Mayo Clinic Health System — "Are you getting too much protein"
2. Harvard Health — "How much protein do you need every day?" (2015, refreshed)
3. NDSU Agriculture — Teens and Protein
4. PTPioneer — Protein Intake Calculator
5. Consumer Reports — Protein powders and lead
6. Mayo Clinic News Network
7. Dr. Stacy Sims — Optimal protein for women
8. NutritionFacts.org — audio
9. CDC Blogs — 2010 post on protein intake
10. MyFitnessPal — Protein Calculator

**Dominant pattern.** Institutional medical sites (Mayo, Harvard, CDC) anchoring the top, all defending or citing the 0.8 g/kg RDA. Word count cluster: 700-1,400 words, mostly prose, very few calculators embedded in the article itself. Citation density: light. Mayo and Harvard rely on authority, not citations — typically 0-3 external references. Freshness: Harvard's flagship URL is dated 2015 with a refresh stamp. CDC post is from 2010. Half the top 10 is stale. Affiliate transparency: not applicable (institutional sites, no affiliate model).

**The gap.** Every top result gives the RDA as the answer. None of them present the muscle-protein-synthesis / resistance-training literature that Peter Attia, Don Layman, Stu Phillips, and Eric Helms have been advancing for a decade. The 1.6-2.2 g/kg range that dominates the optimizer conversation is *absent from the top 10*. Examine.com's calculator ranks only when the query adds "calculator" and even then is outside institutional medicine's grip on the pure head term.

**Weakest displacement target:** CDC Blogs (2010). Stale, prose-only, zero interactive element, ranks on authority alone.

**Opportunity wedge for larderlab:** A single-page tool that (a) reconciles the RDA vs. the optimizer range with a *forked calculator* ("sedentary," "recomp," "hypertrophy," "cut at low BF%," "65+ sarcopenia risk"), (b) cites Morton 2018, Helms 2014, Phillips 2016, Layman's AJCN papers inline, (c) shows the per-meal leucine-threshold math (2.5-3 g), and (d) outputs grams-per-meal for a 3, 4, or 5-meal day. Frame: "The RDA is the floor, not the goal. Here's the actual dose-response curve."

**Dominant pattern.** Affiliate roundup listicles. Typical structure: 2,000-3,000 words, 10-15 products, "tested by our team," minimal testing methodology. Healthline is the authority anchor. Food Network and CNN Underscored are the mass-market anchors. Citation density: zero scientific references. Affiliate transparency: mostly a one-line disclosure above the fold; revenue model is transparent even where the methodology isn't. Freshness: all 2026-dated.

**The gap.** Nobody is answering the *actual* question the optimizer has: "Which material stack minimizes microplastic migration at the temperatures I reheat at?" The entire top 10 treats "BPA-free plastic" as the safety ceiling. Borosilicate is mentioned in passing. Nobody stress-tests the lid gaskets (silicone vs. TPE vs. LDPE). Nobody measures actual fridge-stack footprint. Nobody discusses lid failure modes over time. Nobody does a thermal-shock protocol.

**Weakest displacement target:** Pomodoro Foods / Editorialge. Brand-new domains, thin content, generic product photography, no testing methodology, no author credentials. These rank because of exact-match URL paths and basic on-page SEO — trivial to displace with a methodology page.

**Opportunity wedge for larderlab:** The "Container Stack Protocol" — a decision framework first, product picks second. Header: "Stop buying container sets. Build a stack." Sub-stacks: (a) "reheat daily" (borosilicate only, with thermal-shock test results), (b) "freeze-to-plate weekly" (tempered glass + silicone gasket, with gasket longevity data), (c) "on-the-go lunch" (stainless, gasket-free). Include: a published testing methodology (oven-to-freezer thermal cycle count before failure), a microplastic migration literature summary (Chen 2019, Kontrick 2022), a fridge-stack footprint grid comparison in cm³ per liter of capacity. Cite the Blastocyst study and the FDA's microplastic briefings. No "Bentgo is our pick" until page-scroll 60%.

Word counts: Consumer Reports 2,000-2,500, heavily testing-led; Clean Label Project 1,500 plus embedded database. Citation density on safety sources: strong. On optimization angles: zero. Publish the spreadsheet. Let the user reweight the criteria. No editor's pick — the scorecard is the pick.

### 1.4 "are seed oils bad for you"

**Live top 10 (observed):**
1. Stanford Medicine — Five things to know
2. Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School
3. Houston Methodist — dietitian Q&A
4. NYGA — Truth About Seed Oils
5. UT MD Anderson — Cancerwise
6. Time — "Seed Oils Don't Deserve Their Bad Reputation"
7. AARP
8. Popular Science
9. NPR — 2025 article
10. VaMedReview

**Dominant pattern.** Mainstream medical institutions and legacy media in uniform defense of seed oils. Every article arrives at the same conclusion ("the evidence doesn't support the bad reputation"), usually in 800-1,500 words, low citation density (2-5 refs, mostly to position papers). Authority plays: heavy. Nuance: low. Freshness: strong (2025 cluster).

**The gap.** The entire top 10 is defensive — they're all rebutting the RFK/Attia-adjacent social media narrative. None of them do the actual nuance work: which seed oils at which cooking temperatures with which oxidation products, what the linoleic acid tissue saturation literature actually shows (BaZinga, Ramsden), the difference between refined canola in a deep fryer for 12 hours vs. cold-pressed in a salad dressing, the specific compounds (HNE, 4-HHE, acrolein) that form with reheating. Nobody is doing a smoke-point-to-degradation curve. Nobody publishes the receipts.

**Weakest displacement target:** AARP and VaMedReview. AARP is 1,100 words of generic prose aimed at 55+. VaMedReview is thin. Both rank on domain authority alone.

**Opportunity wedge for larderlab:** Commit to the evidence-forward nuance position that *threads* the needle. Title: "Seed oils: the nuance the defenders and the critics both skip." Structure: (a) tissue incorporation data — what the ratio actually is in adipose at 10%, 7%, 4% linoleic acid (Guyenet's work); (b) oxidation product formation curves by cooking temperature and reuse cycle (cite Rotterdam + 2020 Korean studies); (c) a decision matrix: "For salad dressings, use X. For 180°C sautéing, use Y. For deep-frying at 180°C reused once, use Z." Target featured snippet: "Seed oils are fine at low heat, problematic when reheated, and indistinguishable from processed-food confounding in the epi literature." Cite 15+ primary sources. This is the post that ends the debate — which is why it will collect backlinks from both tribes.

Layer on cost-per-elemental-mg-mg (not cost per pill — elemental magnesium varies 3-5x by form). One-page decision tree, full data appendix below. No editor's pick. The decision tree *is* the pick.

## Section 2: Broader landscape — 10 competitors

These are the brands larderlab competes with for attention inside the evidence-led food systems niche. Not the full SERP competitors — the narrative competitors, the people the target reader already follows.

### 2.1 Examine.com

Model: research-graded supplement reference + paywalled ($29/mo) research feed. Owns the optimizer supplement query at the expert level. Covers zero food/pantry/meal-prep territory — only supplements and a few ingredient queries (creatine, omega-3, magnesium). Voice: clinical, flat, monograph-style. No personality. Strongest citation density on the internet for supplements.

Larderlab's position: adjacent, not competitive. Examine owns supplements; larderlab owns food systems around them. Partner energy, not rivalry. Citation style is the north star to match.

### 2.2 Peter Attia — The Drive / Early Medical

Model: weekly podcast + subscription newsletter (Early Medical, $150/yr). Audience overlap: very high. Attia drives the "2 g/kg protein" conversation in the optimizer space. Content format: long-form interviews + show notes. Zero recipes, zero product recs.

Larderlab's position: the product-layer to Attia's concept-layer. Attia says "eat 1 g of protein per pound." Larderlab shows you how to hit 180 g/day with containers, portions, and a scorecard of protein powders.

### 2.3 Levels Health — blog

Model: CGM hardware + subscription + content arm. Blog has 500+ articles on metabolic health, some food-adjacent ("Foods we love: flaxseeds"). Citation-heavy, clean visual identity, strong brand. Closest competitor to larderlab in *tone*, but Levels leads with glucose data, not food systems.

Larderlab's position: Levels is the glucose lens; larderlab is the pantry lens. Overlap exists on ingredient deep-dives — larderlab needs to out-cite Levels (they average 8-15 refs per post; larderlab should hit 20+).

### 2.4 Stronger By Science — Greg Nuckols, Eric Helms, Eric Trexler

Model: free articles + paywalled research review ($15/mo) + podcast. The highest-rigor resistance-training nutrition publication online. Covers protein dose-response, creatine, caffeine, leucine threshold exhaustively. Zero recipes, zero product recs.

Larderlab's position: Stronger By Science is where the lifter goes for the paper; larderlab is where they go for the grocery cart. Cite them aggressively; link to them; never try to out-academic them on hypertrophy mechanisms.

### 2.5 Lyle McDonald — Body Recomposition

Model: ebooks + long-form blog (slow cadence, but foundational). The original lifter-nutrition evidence hub. Covers protein, carb cycling, flexible dieting. Audience is the 2010s-era RP/IIFYM demographic — directly adjacent to larderlab's reader.

Larderlab's position: modernize Lyle's rigor for a 2026 reader who expects better UX, shorter scannable structures, and mobile-first formatting. Lyle's content lives in walls of text; larderlab's same content lives in decision trees and comparison matrices.

### 2.6 Menno Henselmans

Model: coaching + research-focused blog + courses. Evidence-led lifting nutrition, Euro-coded tone, heavy on meta-analysis summaries. Audience 90%+ male optimizer — exact overlap with larderlab.

Larderlab's position: Menno owns the hypertrophy nutrition angle; larderlab owns the pantry/meal-prep execution layer. Complementary.

### 2.7 Precision Nutrition

Model: coaching certification + content arm. Large volume of generalist nutrition content. Covers meal prep, habit change, client-coaching frameworks. Skews female/general-population, not optimizer. Enterprise feel.

Larderlab's position: Precision Nutrition is the coach's library; larderlab is the reader's operating manual. Very different voice (PN is warm, coaching-coded; larderlab is clinical, systems-coded).

### 2.8 Healthline (nutrition vertical)

Model: ad + affiliate + program-sell (Healthline has a newsletter product). Dominates mid-funnel informational queries ("what is magnesium glycinate," "best time to eat protein"). Medically reviewed, heavy internal linking, 1,500-2,500 word articles. Citation density moderate (5-10 primary sources per long-form), often linking to older reviews.

Larderlab's position: the direct SERP competitor. Larderlab must out-depth, out-cite, and out-specify Healthline on every query they share. Healthline wins on breadth; larderlab wins on depth + optimizer framing.

### 2.9 Garage Gym Reviews

Model: affiliate-driven product review site with strong testing methodology. Owns home-gym equipment queries. Has pushed into protein powder, creatine, and recovery drinks. Testing methodology page is public. This is the *model* for larderlab's product reviews — transparent methodology, published testing protocols, personnel credentials.

Larderlab's position: GGR is the template for how to do evidence-led affiliate reviews well. Borrow the structure; apply it to pantry + kitchen + ingredient queries. Their weakness is they don't touch ingredients, pantry, or food systems — only supplements and equipment.

### 2.10 The Home Edit + Clean Mama (pantry vertical)

Model: product + book + show (The Home Edit is a Netflix brand). Dominant on pantry organization searches at the lifestyle/aesthetic level. Female-coded, aesthetic-first, zero nutritional framing.

Larderlab's position: uncontested positioning. The pantry-organization space has no male-coded, systems-led, nutrition-integrated competitor. Larderlab can own the pantry vertical with zero head-to-head battle because the opposite end of the spectrum is wide open.

## Section 3: Ten concrete positioning moves for larderlab

These are structural wedges, not voice tweaks. Each is a specific format or element that no top-10 competitor ships on the target queries. Priority ordered.

### Move 1: The Decision Tree as hero content

Every money query gets a one-page decision tree *above* the prose. Magnesium, protein powder, rice, cookware, seed oils — all as flowcharts the reader can screenshot. Nobody in the top 10 on any of the six Wave 1 keywords ships a decision tree. The format itself is the differentiator. Investment: one custom Mermaid-or-SVG component in `packages/ui`, reusable across 100+ posts.

### Move 2: Published testing methodology page, linked from every review

Garage Gym Reviews does this for gym equipment. Nobody does it for pantry products, containers, or ingredients. Ship `/methodology/containers`, `/methodology/protein-powder`, `/methodology/pantry-staples`. Each describes exactly how scoring works — what the thermal-shock protocol is, how leucine is calculated, what "third-party tested" means. E-E-A-T grade-A signal.

### Move 3: Reweightable scorecards

Every ranked list gets a user-reweightable scorecard. Reader enters their priorities (taste: 0.1, cost: 0.3, cleanliness: 0.4, protein density: 0.2) → ranking recalculates. Nobody in the SERP does this. Technical lift: small (React component with five sliders). Stickiness + share-ability: very high. Optimizer bait.

### Move 4: "The Floor is Not the Goal" framing on every health-agency recommendation

Every time the RDA or a public-health minimum shows up (protein, magnesium, omega-3, fiber), larderlab opens with "The RDA is the floor. Optimization is the goal." Cite the floor, cite the literature on the optimum, cite the dose-response curve. This is the single biggest wedge against Harvard Health / Mayo — they publish the floor as if it were the answer. The optimizer audience already knows it isn't.

### Move 5: Per-post citation floor of 15 primary sources (not news articles)

Healthline averages 5-10. Mayo averages 0-3. Levels averages 8-15. Larderlab publishes a commitment: every evergreen post has ≥15 primary-source citations (peer-reviewed, ClinicalTrials, FDA, NIH, ODS, EWG, ConsumerLab raw data). Numbered references section. Render as hoverable tooltips with the citation abstract. Nobody in the food vertical does this at this density.

### Move 6: The "receipts appendix"

For contested claims (seed oils, rice arsenic, red meat, fish mercury, protein-kidney), every post ends with an "Addressing the counterclaims" section that quotes the strongest opposing study and steelmans it before rebutting. This is the move that collects backlinks from both tribes of any nutrition debate. No competitor does this — they all pick a side and ignore the opposition.

### Move 7: Male-coded pantry architecture content

Unilaterally claim the pantry vertical with male-coded tonality. "Pantry architecture," not "pantry organization." Zones by macronutrient density, not by food category. Inventory spreadsheet downloads. Bulk-buying break-even calculators. 14-day food security reserves. The Home Edit ends where larderlab starts. Build 20+ posts in this cluster. SERP competition: near-zero on the optimizer-framed queries.

### Move 8: Ingredient deep-dives as pillar pages

One 4,000-6,000 word pillar per "contested ingredient" — seed oils, rice arsenic, fish mercury, red meat, dairy, saturated fat, artificial sweeteners, glyphosate. Each follows the same template: history of the concern, current epidemiological evidence, mechanistic literature, dose-response curves, practical decision matrix, counterclaim steelman, appendix. These are the link-magnet posts. 20-40 references each. Build 12 of them in year one.

### Move 9: Cronometer-native content

The target reader uses Cronometer, not MyFitnessPal. Every macro discussion should reference Cronometer's interface, share export formats, show screenshots of Cronometer dashboards, and publish Cronometer-ready custom food entries (CSV exports readers can import). Nobody in the SERP speaks Cronometer — they all genericize ("use a tracking app"). This is a 10,000-person niche where specificity = loyalty.

### Move 10: Transparent affiliate disclosure that earns trust, not tolerance

Every affiliate post opens with: "We make money when you click the Amazon links below. Here's our testing methodology [link], here's our scoring rubric [link], and here are the products we tested that *didn't* make the list [link]. We're paid to test, not to recommend." Publishing the non-recommended list is the move that earns the optimizer reader's trust. Nobody in the top 10 on any product query does this. Cost: small (maintain a rejected-products page). Signal: enormous.

## Section 5: What larderlab must not become

- **A recipe site.** That's thatcleanchef. If it has a "Serves 4" or "Prep time 20 min," it doesn't belong on larderlab.
- **Personality-driven.** No author-in-the-kitchen videos. No "here's my weekly meal prep." The voice is the editorial team, lab-coded, third-person.
- **Aesthetic-first pantry content.** No "before/after" transformation posts. No chalkboard labels. No clear canister porn.
- **GLP-1-specific nutrition.** That's peptips. Larderlab is drug-naive nutrition.
- **A supplement reference.** Examine owns that. Larderlab covers supplements only where they're pantry-integrated (protein powder, creatine, collagen, magnesium) and only with scorecards, not monographs.
