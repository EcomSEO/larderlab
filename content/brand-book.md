# Larderlab — Brand Book

The voice, audience, and visual identity that every post, pin, and page on larderlab.com must reflect.

---

## 1. Who we are

Larderlab is the most rigorous food systems resource on the internet. We publish buying guides, protein targets, pantry architectures, meal prep frameworks, and ingredient deep-dives — grounded in peer-reviewed research and real-cost math. We don't publish recipes. We don't publish personality. We publish the operating manual for a functional food system.

We are not a blog. We are not a meal plan. We are not "your new favorite food friend." We are the internal wiki your house would have if your house had engineers running it.

Think: Examine.com meets Wirecutter, applied to everything between the grocery list and the plate.

## 2. The reader

### Primary persona — "Mark, 36"

He's a senior engineer at a mid-sized software company. Married, one kid (4 years old), another on the way. Household income $240k-$380k. Lives in Austin or Seattle or the East Bay.

He lifts 4 days a week. He's 185lb and holding, not trying to cut. He tracks protein in Cronometer because he read Phillips 2017 on his own. He listens to The Drive religiously. He's had his ApoB checked. He knows his Lp(a). He's done a DEXA scan.

He already knows that "eat more vegetables" is not the frontier anymore. He's past the beginner content. He wants the next level: which frozen fish has the lowest mercury for the price, what the actual half-life of a bottle of olive oil is, whether jasmine vs basmati actually matters for arsenic, and whether the $40/month his wife spends on Thorne magnesium is justified given the bioavailability data.

He's frustrated by:
- Nutrition blogs written by people who clearly don't track their own macros
- 3,000-word recipe posts where the actual answer is in one line near the bottom
- "Healthy eating" content that stops at "whole foods good, processed bad"
- Evidence-free supplement reviews written in eight minutes by outsourced writers
- Peter Attia being too expensive / podcast-heavy for a quick lookup
- Examine.com being authoritative but dry and poorly structured for skimming
- Reddit threads contradicting each other on the same topic 6 different ways

He trusts:
- Primary literature (he'll open PubMed)
- A handful of specific people: Peter Attia, Rhonda Patrick (cautiously), Menno Henselmans, Stan Efferding, Alan Aragon, Lyle McDonald, Greg Nuckols
- Wirecutter (as a format, not for food)
- Examine.com (as the gold standard of citation density)
- Published per-serving lab data (e.g., CLEAR test reports, Consumer Labs, FDA disclosures)
- Spreadsheets

He does not trust:
- Wellness influencers with affiliate disclosures longer than their articles
- Seed-oil-panic TikToks (despite agreeing with some of them)
- Healthline (too generic, too obviously SEO-farmed)
- Anyone who uses the phrase "superfood"
- Anyone monetized by a supplement they sell
- Generic "eat the rainbow" content

### Secondary personas

- **"Eleanor, 41"** — female engineer/PM equivalent. Same rigor, different frame. Cares more about micronutrient gaps and family logistics. Husband is Mark, essentially. ~30% of audience.
- **"Ryan, 28"** — pre-family, lifts, is optimizing his first real "adult kitchen" from an apartment full of takeout. High LTV if we get him early. ~20% of audience.
- **"Diana, 48"** — second-career professional, has a teenager and a frozen shoulder, wants the macronutrient + supplement basics to hold muscle through perimenopause. ~15% of audience. Gateway to a quieter sub-audience we take seriously without catering to.

We write for Mark by default. The others come along for the ride because the framework is universal even if the examples skew masculine.

## 3. The promise

> We engineer the food system so you don't have to.

Three things every Larderlab post must deliver:

1. **A system, not a vibe.** Every recommendation is framed as "here's the rule, here's the data, here's the decision matrix" — not "here are some ideas to try."
2. **Numbers with sources.** Every claim has a citation with a year, a journal, a sample size when relevant. "Studies show" is banned. "The meta-analysis by Morton et al. 2018 (n=1,863) found..." is the bar.
3. **Real-cost math.** Every buying decision is framed against $/gram of protein, $/day, $/microgram of bioactive compound, or similar. The reader should never leave without a unit-economic view.

## 4. Voice

### How we sound
- **Engineered.** Step-by-step. Flowchart-adjacent. If a post can be a table, it's a table.
- **Cited.** Numbers, DOIs, trial names. "Morton et al. 2018," not "research shows."
- **Skeptical.** Not contrarian. We acknowledge consensus when consensus is real and we flag it when it isn't.
- **Cost-aware.** Every recommendation has a price per unit.
- **Tool-agnostic.** We like Cronometer, MyFitnessPal, MacroFactor. We don't fanboy any of them.
- **Calm.** No alarm, no panic, no "are you sabotaging your gains?" headlines.

### How we don't sound
- ❌ Recipe blog ("Hey fam! Today I'm sharing my FAVORITE high-protein breakfast...")
- ❌ Bro science ("Bros, if you're not hitting 2g/lb of bodyweight you're leaving gains on the table")
- ❌ Wellness-coded ("✨ Nourish your body with these clean, whole-food staples ✨")
- ❌ Trad-food alarmism ("Seed oils are POISON — here's what our great-grandparents actually ate")
- ❌ Snake-oil coded ("This one micronutrient could fix your fatigue OVERNIGHT")
- ❌ Personality-first ("As a busy mama of three who also runs a startup...")

### Side-by-side examples

**❌ Don't write this:**
> Protein is so important, you guys! Make sure you're getting enough every day — aim for about 1 gram per pound of bodyweight. I love starting my day with a delicious protein smoothie! What are your favorite ways to hit your protein goals?

**✅ Write this:**
> The consensus protein target for people who resistance train is 1.6g/kg/day — the conclusion of Morton et al. (2018, *Br J Sports Med*, meta-analysis of 49 studies, n=1,863). Higher intakes (up to 2.2g/kg) show diminishing returns; lower intakes (≤1.2g/kg) consistently underperform on lean mass retention. For an 80kg lifter, that's 128g/day. Hitting it with 3 meals means ~43g per meal, which is ~150g cooked chicken or ~300g Greek yogurt. Cheapest sources by $/gram of protein: eggs in bulk ($0.02/g), chicken thighs at Costco ($0.015/g), whey concentrate ($0.013/g). We track this against 4 other target ranges and 12 source options in the Protein Calculator.

---

**❌ Don't write this:**
> Seed oils have been making headlines lately and for good reason. These industrial oils are everywhere and they might be contributing to a lot of modern health problems. Here's what you need to know to make informed choices.

**✅ Write this:**
> The seed oil debate has three layers, and most online content conflates them. Layer 1: linoleic acid intake ratios (actual data: Western intake has risen from ~2% to ~7% of energy over 100 years — Blasbalg et al. 2011). Layer 2: oxidation products from repeated high-heat frying (real concern, limited human trials, Vladykovskaya et al. 2012). Layer 3: "seed oils cause inflammation" (weak evidence in humans for isolated linoleic acid; Johnson & Fritsche 2012 meta-analysis found no significant effect on inflammatory markers). Most public discourse is arguing Layer 3 while citing Layer 2 studies. Our position: limit seed oils from restaurant fryers (Layer 2 is real), don't stress about the 1 tablespoon in your salad dressing (Layer 3 is overstated). ## 5. Visual identity

### Colors
- **Primary**: Ink `#1A2332` (deep midnight, not quite black)
- **Accent**: Paper `#F5F2EB` (off-white with warmth)
- **Highlight**: Oxidized copper `#B87333` (for callouts, accents, the occasional pull element)
- **Secondary**: Steel `#6B7A8A` (for meta, dividers, second-tier text)
- **Text**: Charcoal `#202020` on paper, never pure black

Color philosophy: materials of a working kitchen, but without the warmth of a cookbook. Think: the well-organized interior of a chef's walk-in, not grandma's pantry.

### Typography
- **Headlines**: A modern serif with engineering discipline. Recommend **Söhne Breit** or **GT Alpina** if budget allows. Fallback: **Fraunces** (smaller, tighter settings than plasticfreelab — this isn't a softer brand).
- **Body**: A neutral sans with good monospace fallback available. Recommend **Söhne** or **Inter Tight**. Body at 17px, line-height 1.55 — tighter than plasticfreelab because readers skim tables and code-like content.
- **Tables, calculators, numerical callouts**: **IBM Plex Mono** or **JetBrains Mono**. The numerical content IS the content — treat it typographically.
- **Captions and citations**: Same sans, smaller, slightly muted

### Photography style
- **Flat-lay overhead.** Grids of ingredients, pantry contents, supplement bottles, kitchen tools — shot from directly above on a neutral ground.
- **No people.** Ever. Not hands, not blurred bodies, not faces.
- **Material-focused.** Glass, stainless, wood, linen, brushed metal. No plastic, no bright branding.
- **Daylight only, neutral cast.** No warm Instagram filter. No moody dark shots either. Clean, slightly cool, daylit.
- **Deliberately repetitive.** The same camera angle and background across a hub creates a visual index — like specimen plates.
- **No styling food for food's sake.** A bowl of rice is photographed for what it is. We don't sprinkle thyme for the shot.

### What our pages look like
- Lots of whitespace around dense tables
- Fixed-width monospace for numbers, inline code-style
- Calculators embedded as real interactive components (not a screenshot of a google sheet)
- Citation footnotes that collapse inline when clicked
- Tables are first-class design elements — we engineer them, not just render them
- Zero ads in months 1-12. Possibly forever for this site — the affiliate model is strong enough.
- Single-column reading width, max ~680px for prose, tables breakout wider

### What we don't look like
- Food blog (florals, script fonts, "Jump to Recipe" button, food emoji, pastels)
- Bodybuilder forum (high-contrast, neon accents, "SHREDDED" visual language)
- Clinical health site (stock photo lab coat, blue, sans serifs, sterile)
- Biohacker (red light aesthetic, sci-fi gradients, chrome + black)
- Supplement retailer (product shots, buy buttons, CTAs everywhere)

## 6. Editorial principles (larderlab-specific, on top of network principles)

1. **Numbers or nothing.** Any claim in the body has to be backed by a number with a citation. "High protein" is unacceptable; "1.6g/kg/day (Morton 2018)" is acceptable. If a claim resists numerical translation, it probably shouldn't be in the post.

2. **Tables over paragraphs.** When information is comparative (protein sources, oils, supplement brands, storage shelf lives), the table is the answer. The paragraph introduces the table and says what to do with it.

3. **Cost per unit, always.** $/gram of protein, $/gram of omega-3, $/microgram of B12, $/serving. Never just list products without a unit-economic take.

4. **Declare the consensus and the dissent.** When literature is mixed, we say so in a callout: "Consensus position: X. Notable dissenters: Y (Z paper). Our read: A. Reason: B." Never pretend certainty we don't have.

5. **No "best" without criteria.** Every ranking spells out the criteria before the ranking. "Best protein powder" isn't useful — "Best protein powder for $/g of leucine with no artificial sweeteners" is.

6. **Steel-man the alternative.** When we recommend against a product, category, or practice, we articulate the strongest version of the other side's argument, then address it.

7. **Update on primary-source drift.** If a cited study gets retracted, a meta-analysis is updated, or a FDA guidance changes — the affected posts get a note + a dated revision. Version history stays visible.

8. **One original observation per section.** Every H2 contains at least one observation, calculation, or framing that isn't pulled from the sources. This is what differentiates Larderlab from a glorified summary of PubMed.

9. **No "scroll of shame." No listicles longer than 15 items.** Listicles are a Larderlab format too, but they're engineered — 8-12 items max, each with a reason to exist.

10. **We never write "superfood," "clean eating," "toxin," "detox," "miracle," or "hack" in editorial.** These words signal audience confusion and we don't confuse our reader.

## 9. Reader journey

1. **Reader Googles a specific question** — protein target, a supplement, an ingredient, a meal prep framework. Larderlab ranks because the post is densely cited and structurally better than the alternatives.
2. **Reader lands on a calculator, a table, or a deep-dive** — gets the actual answer in the first screen. Notes that the footer has 23 citations.
3. **Reader subscribes** — the lead magnet is a real spreadsheet they want to use. "The Larderlab Macro Planner" (free Google Sheet) or "The Pantry Architecture Template" (downloadable Notion/Obsidian doc).
4. **Reader returns** — because the next question also leads back here.
5. **Reader buys through an affiliate link** — protein powder, a magnesium brand, a meal delivery service, a food storage system. The affiliate click is high-intent because the recommendation came with receipts.
6. **Long-term: reader becomes a buyer** of a house digital product (the full Macro Planner, the Protein Database subscription, an ebook).

The lead magnet for Wave 1: **"The Larderlab Macro Planner"** — a Google Sheet (or Notion template) that calculates protein/fat/carb targets for the user's bodyweight, splits them across 3-5 meals, and generates a $/gram cost chart for 20 common protein sources. Free. Functional. Spreadsheet-literate.

## 10. Distribution strategy

Pinterest is a secondary channel for Larderlab — the visual identity doesn't translate as well as plasticfreelab. Primary distribution:

- **Google search** — the main channel. Pillar pages and calculators.
- **Reddit** — calculator posts and data-dense deep-dives get shared organically on r/nutrition, r/loseit, r/AdvancedFitness, r/SaturatedFat, etc. Don't force it, but write so it happens.
- **Twitter/X** — single-post shares in the Attia/Patrick/Huberman comment ecosystem when data is publication-worthy.
- **Email** — weekly digest to subscribers. One useful thing per week, always a number.
- **Pinterest (secondary)** — high-contrast tables and calculators can work. "Protein target by bodyweight, visualized." Not personality pins.

### Pin formats (secondary)
- **Standard pins**: 1000×1500, a table or chart as the hero, minimal text overlay
- **Calculator screenshot pins**: the calculator UI as the visual
- **Chart pins**: a single data visualization as the pin (e.g., "$/gram of protein, 20 sources, ranked")

### Pin headline formulas (restrained — we don't do clickbait)
- *Protein sources, ranked by $/gram*
- *The magnesium forms, compared*
- *[N] pantry staples that beat the bulk-buy math*
- *How much [nutrient] per day — by bodyweight*

## 12. The line we never cross

Larderlab exists because the evidence-led nutrition space is dominated by people monetized by what they're recommending. The moment our rankings get influenced by affiliate payout rates, we lose the reason the reader came.

Specifically:
- We do not accept payment from any supplement brand for a ranking position
- We do not accept "sample packs" as compensation — we buy what we review at retail price
- We do not publish sponsored content under the editorial byline (sponsored must be labeled, visually distinct, and is not used in months 1-12)
- We disclose every affiliate relationship on every page
- We show the price we paid, dated, on every product we review
- When our top pick changes, we annotate the previous ranking and date the change

Trust, in this niche, is denominated in citation density and cost disclosure. That's the moat.
